Resources for Caregivers of Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Caring for an adult with cerebral palsy is demanding—physically, emotionally, and financially. Caregiver burnout is real and common. Taking care of yourself is essential for providing sustainable care.

Caregiver Burden​

Research shows that caregiver burden is associated with:

  • Caregiver depression - Mental health directly affects burden
  • Severity of disability - More support needs = more caregiver strain

"Children with more severe disabilities require the more support of their caregivers."

This applies equally to adults—more complex care needs create more caregiver burden.

Warning Signs of Burnout​

Watch for these signs in yourself:

  • Exhaustion that doesn't improve with rest
  • Feeling overwhelmed or hopeless
  • Withdrawal from activities you once enjoyed
  • Resentment toward the person you care for
  • Neglecting your own health
  • Sleep problems
  • Increased illness
  • Difficulty concentrating

Physical Care Demands​

Caregiving for adults with cerebral palsy often involves:

Daily Tasks​

  • Transfers (bed, chair, toilet, vehicle)
  • Bathing and hygiene assistance
  • Dressing
  • Feeding or meal preparation
  • Medication management
  • Equipment maintenance

Body Mechanics​

Protect yourself from injury:

  • Learn proper lifting and transfer techniques
  • Use adaptive equipment (Hoyer lifts, transfer boards)
  • Ask for help with heavy lifting
  • See a physical therapist for training
  • Replace equipment before it fails

Emotional and Mental Health​

Your Mental Health Matters​

Research shows caregiver depression directly increases burden.
Addressing your own mental health:

  • Is not selfish—it's necessary
  • Improves your caregiving ability
  • Models healthy behavior
  • Prevents burnout

Getting Support​

  • Therapy/counseling - Individual or caregiver-focused
  • Support groups - In-person or online communities
  • Respite care - Regular breaks are essential
  • Peer support - Connect with other caregivers

Respite Care Options​

Everyone needs breaks. Options include:

TypeDescription
In-home respiteProvider comes to your home
Adult day programsDaytime activities and supervision
Overnight respiteTemporary residential care
Family/friend reliefTrained loved ones providing breaks

Finding Respite​

  • Ask the physiatrist or care coordinator
  • Contact local United Cerebral Palsy chapter
  • Check Medicaid waiver programs
  • Explore state developmental disabilities services
  • Ask at local churches or community organizations

Financial Resources​

Government Programs​

  • Medicaid waivers - May cover attendant care, equipment, respite
  • SSI/SSDI - Income support for person with disability
  • Medicare - For those who qualify
  • State disability services - Vary by state

Caregiver-Specific​

  • FMLA - Job-protected leave for caregiving
  • Caregiver tax credits - Dependent care deductions
  • Local assistance - Many communities have emergency funds

Charitable Resources​

  • United Cerebral Palsy
  • Easter Seals
  • Local disability organizations
  • Disease-specific foundations
  • Religious organizations

Building Your Support Network​

Professional Team​

Ensure the care team is complete:

  • Physiatrist as coordinator
  • Primary care physician
  • Therapists (PT, OT, SLP)
  • Home health aides
  • Care coordinator (if available)

Personal Network​

  • Family members who can help
  • Friends willing to learn care tasks
  • Neighbors for emergencies
  • Faith community
  • Online communities

Self-Care Strategies​

Physical Health​

  • Maintain your own medical appointments
  • Get adequate sleep (even if it requires respite)
  • Exercise regularly
  • Eat properly
  • Don't ignore symptoms

Mental Health​

  • Schedule time for yourself daily
  • Maintain hobbies and interests
  • Stay connected with friends
  • Consider therapy
  • Practice stress management

Boundaries​

  • Learn to say no
  • Accept that you cannot do everything
  • Ask for and accept help
  • Recognize when you need a break

When Caregiving Is No Longer Sustainable​

Sometimes home caregiving becomes unsustainable. This may be due to:

  • Caregiver health decline
  • Increasing care complexity
  • Physical demands exceeding ability
  • Burnout despite interventions

Options to explore:

  • Increased in-home support
  • Supported living arrangements
  • Group homes
  • Skilled nursing facilities

Recognizing limits is not failure—it's responsible care planning.

Key Points​

  • Caregiver burden is real and measurable
  • Your mental health directly affects caregiving sustainability
  • Respite is not optional—it's necessary
  • Build a support network before crisis
  • Physical self-care prevents injury
  • Financial resources exist—explore all options
  • Knowing your limits protects everyone
