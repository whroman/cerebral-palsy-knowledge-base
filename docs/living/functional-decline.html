Functional Decline in Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Although the brain injury that causes cerebral palsy does not worsen over time, adults commonly experience progressive decline in physical function. This is not inevitable—understanding the patterns and causes allows for prevention and intervention.

How Common Is Decline?​

Functional decline affects the majority of adults with cerebral palsy: [Citation not found: pubmed-38433033]

  • 60% report decline in gross motor function since childhood [Citation not found: pubmed-38433033]
  • 25% or more of ambulant adults experience gait decline
  • 40% of adults who walked in adolescence lost this ability over two decades [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]
  • 50% report decreased daily functional activities by the fourth decade [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

Decline increases with age across all domains. [Citation not found: pubmed-38433033]

Timeline of Changes​

Late 20s to Early 30s​

Most noticeable decrements in strength and functional reserve begin appearing. [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

Age 35+​

Ambulatory function typically begins declining. [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

10-14 Year Follow-Up Data​

In a longitudinal study of adults aged 35-45: [Citation not found: pubmed-28427924]

What Causes Decline?​

Functional decline results from multiple interacting factors: [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

Musculoskeletal Changes​

  • Sarcopenia (muscle loss) occurs earlier than in the general population
  • Osteoporosis and vitamin D deficiency (>50% of adults) [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]
  • Increased risk of fragility fractures
  • Progressive joint contractures and deformities

Chronic Pain and Fatigue​

  • 75% experience persistent chronic pain [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]
  • 32% report severe fatigue [Citation not found: pubmed-28427924]
  • Pain and fatigue directly contribute to reduced activity

Cardiometabolic Conditions​

Adults with cerebral palsy have higher rates of: [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

  • Diabetes
  • Hypertension
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Metabolic syndrome (20-29% prevalence) [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

Swallowing Dysfunction​

Gradual deterioration in swallowing begins as early as age 30, with increased aspiration risks despite maintained dietary patterns. [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]

Risk Factors for Greater Decline​

Higher risk of functional decline occurs in those who:

  • Are older
  • Have less independent gait
  • Have bilateral motor impairment
  • Experience higher levels of pain
  • Have higher GMFCS levels

Prevention and Intervention​

Regular Monitoring​

"Systematic follow-up of adults with cerebral palsy appears necessary" [Citation not found: pubmed-28427924]

Annual or regular assessments should track:

  • Gross motor function changes
  • Pain levels
  • Fatigue
  • Walking ability
  • Strength

Physical Activity​

Maintaining physical activity helps preserve function. Exercise programs should be:

  • Adapted to current abilities
  • Sustainable long-term
  • Focused on strength and endurance

Pain Management​

Addressing chronic pain reduces its impact on function and activity levels. Options include:

  • Medication management
  • Physical therapy
  • Botulinum toxin injections
  • Intrathecal baclofen for spasticity

Nutrition and Bone Health​

  • Screen for vitamin D deficiency
  • Address calcium intake
  • Monitor bone density
  • Prevent malnutrition

Assistive Technology​

Timely introduction of mobility aids and assistive devices can preserve participation even as function changes.

What To Expect​

Functional decline does not mean loss of quality of life. With appropriate support: [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

  • Adaptation strategies maintain participation
  • Assistive technology compensates for physical changes
  • Self-awareness helps manage symptoms
  • Planning ahead reduces crisis situations

Adults aging with cerebral palsy describe developing acceptance of change while taking charge of the help they need. [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

When to Seek Help​

Contact your healthcare provider if you notice:

  • New difficulty with activities you could previously do
  • Increased pain or fatigue
  • Changes in walking pattern or endurance
  • Swallowing difficulties
  • Falls or near-falls

Early intervention can slow or reverse some aspects of decline.

Key Points​

  • 60% of adults with cerebral palsy report gross motor decline since childhood [Citation not found: pubmed-38433033]
  • Decline is not from worsening brain injury—it's from secondary changes
  • Major contributors: pain, fatigue, sarcopenia, inactivity
  • Decline accelerates after age 35 but signs appear in late 20s [Citation not found: pmc-6637058]
  • Regular monitoring and early intervention can preserve function
  • Assistive technology and adaptation maintain quality of life
