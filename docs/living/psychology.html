Psychology and Identity in Cerebral Palsy

Living with cerebral palsy shapes not just the body but the inner life—how people see themselves, relate to others, and make meaning of their experiences across the lifespan.

This page addresses the psychological experience of being a person with cerebral palsy, distinct from clinical mental health disorders like depression and anxiety.

Identity Development​

Disability identity is a unique phenomenon that shapes how people see themselves, their bodies, and their way of interacting with the world. [Citation not found: pubmed-28406650]

How Identity Forms​

Identity develops through three key mechanisms: [Citation not found: pubmed-28406650]

  • Mirroring - seeing oneself reflected in others' responses
  • Modeling - observing others with disabilities
  • Recognition - through available identity resources (communities, role models, narratives)

Identity development is fundamentally a social process. [Citation not found: pubmed-28406650] This means the attitudes of family, peers, and professionals profoundly shape how a person with cerebral palsy comes to understand themselves.

The Identity Tension​

Adults with cerebral palsy face a critical tension in how they construct their identity: [Citation not found: pubmed-25176001]

Identity EmphasisBenefitCost
Personal identity (unique individual)Receiving individualized supportSupport eligibility may be questioned
Social identity (disability group member)Access to support, coping resourcesStigmatization, feeling different

Half of adults seeking support experience stigma in those settings. [Citation not found: pubmed-25176001] People navigate this by shifting between identity emphases depending on context and need.

Cerebral Palsy as One Part of Self​

In adulthood, many people work to integrate cerebral palsy into a broader sense of self rather than letting it define them entirely: [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

"I don't know how to bridge that gap between 'this is who I am' and 'this is me with cerebral palsy.'"

Participants in aging studies noted that social attitudes and stigma often proved more disabling than the condition itself. [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

Self-Concept Across the Lifespan​

Self-concept—how people evaluate and feel about themselves—follows distinct patterns across development.

Early Childhood​

Children with cerebral palsy begin to regard themselves as different as early as four years of age. However, these self-views and their potentially negative effects on self-esteem do not appear to crystallize until the primary school years. [Citation not found: pubmed-16417673]

Adolescence​

A systematic review found insufficient evidence that children with cerebral palsy in general have lower global self-concept than peers without disability. [Citation not found: pubmed-16417673]

However, adolescent females represent an at-risk group with significantly lower self-concept in four domains: [Citation not found: pubmed-16417673]

DomainEffect Size
Social acceptance-1.24 (large)
Physical appearance-1.16 (large)
Athletic competence-0.93 (large)
Scholastic competence-0.86 (large)

Adulthood​

In adults with cerebral palsy, self-concept is not related to motor function or disability severity. [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]

Self-concept is related to: [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]

  • Depression (inverse relationship)
  • Life satisfaction (positive relationship)

Among 64 adults studied: [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]

  • 16% had high self-concept [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]
  • 64% had average self-concept [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]
  • 20% had low self-concept [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]

For those with low self-concept, family and personal relationship domains were most affected. [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]

Body Image and Self-Awareness​

Body Image​

Adolescents with cerebral palsy show a less favorable body image compared to peers without disability. [Citation not found: pubmed-26754030]

For young people constantly reminded of physical restrictions, experiencing body vitality—a sense of the body as capable and alive—becomes especially important. Healthcare professionals' interactions can either support or undermine positive body image.

Bodily Self-Awareness​

Self-awareness of one's own body emerges as a critical skill for managing cerebral palsy across the lifespan. [Citation not found: pubmed-23614354]

"Self-awareness appears to be an important process to be fostered by service providers and parents."

People who develop strong body awareness can: [Citation not found: pubmed-23614354]

  • Recognize early signs of fatigue
  • Identify pain patterns before they escalate
  • Adapt activities to maintain participation
  • Use strategies like rest breaks and equipment proactively

Some people lack awareness of fatigue indicators and adaptation techniques—a gap that transition programs should address. [Citation not found: pubmed-23614354]

Lived Experience Across Life Stages​

Youth​

Young people with cerebral palsy experience disruptions to their life trajectories through physical limitations, yet demonstrate resilience. They develop strategies to revise their biographies to maintain personal and social normalcy. [Citation not found: pubmed-26754030]

Key challenges include: [Citation not found: pubmed-26754030]

  • Pain and unpredictability of body function
  • Fatigue management
  • Navigating accessibility and accommodations
  • Balancing the reality of disability with typical youth identity

Young Adulthood​

The transition to adulthood brings unexpected challenges. Young adults with cerebral palsy often feel unprepared and abandoned by the healthcare system. [Citation not found: pubmed-26754030]

Common experiences include:

  • Psychosocial challenges in relationships and employment
  • Striving toward independence while managing increased physical demands
  • Encountering adult healthcare providers who lack cerebral palsy expertise

Aging​

Adults aging with cerebral palsy describe a growing awareness that physical resources are limited—increased fatigue, diminishing energy, and general slowing down occurring at earlier ages than non-disabled peers. [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

Five key themes characterize the aging experience: [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]

  1. Acceptance of change - adapting through modified routines, rest, equipment
  2. Exploring identity - cerebral palsy as one part of self
  3. Taking charge of help - maintaining autonomy while receiving assistance
  4. Rethinking the future - managing uncertainty about progressive changes
  5. Interacting with health professionals - valuing those who see the whole person

"I need some things done for me but that's different from 'I need to be taken care of.'"

Social Belonging and Participation​

Adults with cerebral palsy experience restricted social participation due to: [Citation not found: pubmed-37497638]

  • Inadequate support during childhood
  • Lifespan physical limitations
  • Societal barriers and expectations
  • Inaccessible environments

Participation is lowest in the domain of intimate relationships. [Citation not found: pubmed-37497638]

What Supports Participation​

  • Age-appropriate support during childhood (critical for adult outcomes) [Citation not found: pubmed-37497638]
  • Fostering autonomy and independence early [Citation not found: pubmed-37497638]
  • Accessible physical environments
  • Social connections with both disabled and non-disabled peers [Citation not found: pubmed-25176001]

Supporting Psychological Well-Being​

For Healthcare Providers​

  • See the whole person, not just the disability [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]
  • Listen to and respect lived experience [Citation not found: pmc-5073482]
  • Tailor services individually [Citation not found: pubmed-25176001]
  • Foster body awareness and self-management skills [Citation not found: pubmed-23614354]
  • Monitor adolescent females for self-concept concerns [Citation not found: pubmed-16417673]

For Families​

  • Support identity development through exposure to disability role models and communities [Citation not found: pubmed-28406650]
  • Continue family-centered care into adulthood [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]
  • Balance protection with fostering independence
  • Validate the person's experience while encouraging adaptation

For Adults with Cerebral Palsy​

  • Seek connection with others who share similar experiences [Citation not found: pubmed-25176001]
  • Develop body awareness and personalized management strategies [Citation not found: pubmed-23614354]
  • Recognize that self-concept is not determined by motor function [Citation not found: pubmed-20804447]
  • Prepare for transitions with realistic expectations and support systems

Key Points​

  • Disability identity develops through social processes—mirroring, modeling, and recognition
  • Self-concept in adults is unrelated to motor severity but connected to depression and life satisfaction
  • Adolescent females are at particular risk for lower self-concept
  • Body awareness is a learnable skill that improves quality of life
  • Social participation depends heavily on childhood support and environmental accessibility
  • Adults aging with cerebral palsy face earlier physical decline but develop adaptive strategies
