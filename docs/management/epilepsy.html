Epilepsy & Seizure Management in Cerebral Palsy

Epilepsy is one of the most common co-occurring conditions in cerebral palsy, affecting about half of all individuals. Understanding seizure risks and management is essential for comprehensive care.

Prevalence​

FindingStatisticSource
Epilepsy in adults with cerebral palsy/intellectual disability55.6%
Epilepsy in large cerebral palsy population (N=88,138)51%
Drug-resistant epilepsy22.4%
Seizure recurrence after medication withdrawal42.3%

Nearly 1 in 2 people with cerebral palsy has epilepsy.

Risk Factors by Cerebral Palsy Subtype​

A large-scale population study of 88,138 patients found that specific cerebral palsy subtypes carry different epilepsy risks:

SubtypeEpilepsy Risk
Spastic diplegicHighest risk (OR = 1.88)
Spastic quadriplegicElevated risk
DyskineticElevated risk
HemiplegicModerate risk
AtaxicLower risk

"Spastic diplegic cerebral palsy demonstrated the strongest correlation with seizure disorders."

Other Risk Factors​

  • Perinatal infection (OR = 1.61)
  • Abnormal brain imaging
  • More severe intellectual disability
  • More severe neurological involvement

Drug-Resistant Epilepsy​

22.4% of individuals with epilepsy and cerebral palsy have drug-resistant epilepsy—meaning seizures are not controlled despite trying multiple medications.

Implications:

  • May need specialist epilepsy center evaluation
  • Consider alternative treatments (vagus nerve stimulation, surgery evaluation)
  • Focus on seizure safety and quality of life
  • Higher care burden for caregivers

Medication Withdrawal Considerations​

Attempting to stop seizure medications carries significant risk:

"42.3% of the individuals who attempted antiseizure medication withdrawal experienced recurrence."

Key findings:

  • Nearly half experience seizure recurrence when attempting withdrawal
  • EEG findings predict relapse risk
  • Decisions should involve neurologist experienced with cerebral palsy
  • Benefits of stopping (reduced side effects) must be weighed against recurrence risk

Management Approach​

Initial Evaluation​

  1. Confirm epilepsy diagnosis - Not all movements in cerebral palsy are seizures
  2. EEG monitoring - Helps characterize seizure type
  3. Brain imaging - MRI to assess structural causes
  4. Review of all medications - Interactions with cerebral palsy treatments

Medication Selection​

Considerations specific to cerebral palsy:

  • Avoid medications that worsen spasticity (some may help it)
  • Consider sedation effects in those with cognitive impairment
  • Watch for bone density effects (many anticonvulsants reduce bone density)
  • Account for swallowing difficulties (liquid vs. tablet formulations)
  • Interactions with baclofen, botulinum toxin, and other cerebral palsy medications

Specialist Team​

SpecialistRole
NeurologistPrimary seizure management
PhysiatristCoordinate with spasticity treatment
EpileptologistDrug-resistant cases
PharmacistMedication interactions

Safety Considerations​

For adults with cerebral palsy and epilepsy:

  • Supervision during bathing - Drowning risk with seizures
  • Fall prevention - Combined mobility issues and seizure risk
  • Medication adherence support - Especially if cognitive impairment
  • Caregiver seizure training - Recognition and response
  • Emergency medication access - Rescue medications if prescribed

Key Points​

  • About 51-56% of people with cerebral palsy have epilepsy

  • Spastic diplegic cerebral palsy has the highest epilepsy risk
  • 22.4% have drug-resistant epilepsy
  • Medication withdrawal leads to seizure recurrence in nearly half who attempt it
  • Management requires coordination between neurologist and physiatrist
  • Cerebral palsy subtype and early medical history inform epilepsy risk
