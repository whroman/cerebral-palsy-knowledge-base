Spasticity Management in Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Spasticity—increased muscle tone causing stiffness and resistance to movement—is a core feature of cerebral palsy that requires ongoing management throughout life.

Why Spasticity Management Matters​

Unmanaged spasticity leads to:

  • Chronic pain (affects 70% of adults)
  • Contractures and deformities
  • Reduced function and independence
  • Difficulty with positioning and caregiving
  • Sleep disruption

Treatment Hierarchy​

Spasticity management follows a stepped approach from least to most invasive:

1. Physical Interventions (First-Line)​

  • Stretching - Regular range of motion exercises
  • Positioning - Proper seating, sleeping positions
  • Orthotics - Splints, braces to maintain position
  • Physical therapy - Strengthening, functional training

2. Oral Medications​

Oral medications reduce generalized spasticity but have limited evidence and significant side effects.

"The American Academy of Neurology states there is insufficient evidence to support or refute the effectiveness of oral baclofen for cerebral palsy spasticity."

MedicationMechanismCommon Side Effects
BaclofenGABAB receptor agonistDrowsiness, weakness, tolerance
TizanidineAlpha-2 agonistDrowsiness, dry mouth
DiazepamGABA enhancerSedation, dependence
DantroleneMuscle relaxantHepatotoxicity, weakness

"Although oral baclofen is not as effective for cerebral spasticity as it is for spinal spasticity, it remains commonly prescribed."

Managing Side Effects​

  • Drowsiness - Administer doses at bedtime
  • Tolerance (3-20% of patients) - "Baclofen holidays" of 3-37 days can restore effectiveness
  • Constipation - Manageable with laxatives

3. Botulinum Toxin Injections​

For focal spasticity affecting specific muscle groups:

"Three Class I trials documented significant improvement in gait pattern in children with gastrocnemius spasticity."

  • Targets specific muscles
  • Effects last 3-6 months
  • Requires repeat injections
  • "Adverse events were mild and self-limited. The most common complaints were pain in the injection sites and transient weakness."

Best for:

  • Equinovarus foot deformity
  • Specific muscle groups causing functional problems
  • Combined with stretching and casting

4. Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB)​

For severe, generalized spasticity not controlled by other methods:

A meta-analysis found "average spasticity scores decreased from 3.2 to 1.9, representing a 40.25% reduction" with ITB therapy.

Benefits ​

  • 40% spasticity reduction
  • Modest motor function improvement (9.62%)
  • Continuous delivery directly to spinal cord
  • Avoids oral medication side effects

Risks ​

Complications in 203 of 501 implantations:

  • Infections: 16.26%
  • Meningitis: 3.94%
  • Catheter problems: 75 cases

Requires:

  • Surgical implantation of pump
  • Regular refills (every 1-6 months)
  • Lifelong maintenance
  • Emergency access if pump fails

5. Surgical Options​

For severe cases or established deformities:

  • Selective dorsal rhizotomy - Cutting nerve rootlets
  • Orthopedic surgery - Tendon lengthening, osteotomies

Specialist Roles​

SpecialistRole in Spasticity Management
PhysiatristPrimary coordinator, oral medications, botulinum toxin
NeurologistComplex medication management
OrthopedistSurgical interventions
Physical therapistStretching, strengthening, positioning
NeurosurgeonITB pump placement, selective dorsal rhizotomy

Key Points​

  • Start with physical interventions before medications
  • Oral medications have limited evidence but can help generalized spasticity
  • Botulinum toxin is effective for focal spasticity affecting specific muscles
  • Intrathecal baclofen provides significant reduction for severe cases but requires surgical implantation
  • A physiatrist should coordinate spasticity management as part of overall care
  • Regular reassessment needed—spasticity patterns change with aging
