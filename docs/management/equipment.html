Equipment and Assistive Devices for Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Proper equipment can dramatically improve independence, comfort, and quality of life. Equipment needs often change with aging, making regular reassessment essential.

Categories of Equipment​

Mobility Devices​

Wheelchairs​

Manual Wheelchairs

  • Self-propelled or attendant-pushed
  • Lightweight options for active users
  • Custom seating for positioning needs
  • Lower cost, no charging required

Power Wheelchairs

  • Essential when manual propulsion isn't possible
  • Various control options (joystick, head array, sip-and-puff)
  • Tilt/recline features for pressure relief
  • Elevating seats for access and transfers

Walkers and Gait Aids​

  • Posterior walkers - better posture than anterior walkers
  • Rollators - wheeled walkers with seats
  • Crutches - forearm or platform styles
  • Canes - for mild balance support

Standers​

  • Enable upright positioning
  • Benefits: bone density, digestion, circulation
  • Allow hands-free activities
  • Prevent contractures from prolonged sitting

Orthotics​

"Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) reduce energy expenditure during ambulation and prevent joint deformities."

TypePurpose
AFOs (Ankle-Foot Orthoses)Foot/ankle positioning, gait improvement
SMOs (Supra-Malleolar Orthoses)Heel and foot support, shoe inserts
Knee orthosesKnee stability and alignment
Hand/wrist splintsImprove grasp, prevent contractures
Trunk orthosesScoliosis management, posture support

Getting Orthotics​

  1. Referral from physiatrist or orthopedist
  2. Evaluation by certified orthotist
  3. Casting or scanning for custom fit
  4. Fitting and adjustments
  5. Regular reassessment (fit changes over time)

Communication Devices (AAC)​

Augmentative and Alternative Communication devices for those with speech difficulties:

Low-Tech Options

  • Communication boards
  • Picture exchange systems
  • Writing aids

High-Tech Options

  • Speech-generating devices
  • Tablet apps with voice output
  • Eye-gaze systems
  • Switch-activated devices

Daily Living Aids​

  • Eating - Adapted utensils, plate guards, non-slip mats
  • Dressing - Button hooks, zipper pulls, dressing sticks
  • Bathing - Shower chairs, grab bars, long-handled sponges
  • Toileting - Raised toilet seats, grab bars, bidets
  • Writing - Weighted pens, grip aids, slant boards

Computer Access​

  • Alternative keyboards (large key, one-handed)
  • Mouse alternatives (trackball, joystick, head mouse)
  • Voice recognition software
  • Switch scanning for those with limited movement
  • Eye-tracking systems

Funding Equipment​

Insurance​

  • Medicare/Medicaid may cover medically necessary equipment
  • Requires prescription and documentation of medical necessity
  • Prior authorization often required
  • May require specific suppliers

Vocational Rehabilitation​

  • Equipment needed for employment
  • Varies by state
  • Can cover high-cost items like power wheelchairs

Charitable Organizations​

  • United Cerebral Palsy chapters
  • Easter Seals
  • Condition-specific foundations
  • Local civic organizations

Equipment Lending Libraries​

Before purchasing expensive equipment, many organizations offer:

  • Demo equipment to try
  • Short-term loans
  • Refurbished equipment at reduced cost

Working with Specialists​

SpecialistRole
PhysiatristPrescribes equipment, coordinates team
Physical TherapistMobility assessment, training
Occupational TherapistADL equipment, upper extremity aids
Speech-Language PathologistAAC evaluation and training
OrthotistCustom braces and orthoses
ATP (Assistive Technology Professional)Complex wheelchair seating
Rehabilitation EngineerCustom modifications

Equipment Updates with Aging​

Equipment needs change with age:

  • Increased fatigue → May need power mobility earlier
  • Reduced strength → Lighter equipment, power assists
  • Pain → Better positioning, pressure management
  • Changing body → Refitting orthotics, new seating

Regular reassessment is essential—what worked at 20 may not work at 40.

Key Points​

  • Equipment should be prescribed by appropriate specialists
  • Try before you buy when possible
  • Custom fitting is essential for orthotics and wheelchairs
  • Multiple funding sources may be needed
  • Needs change with aging—plan for reassessment
  • Good equipment improves independence and quality of life
  • Poor fit or wrong equipment can cause harm
