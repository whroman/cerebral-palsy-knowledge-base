Pain Management in Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Chronic pain affects the majority of adults with cerebral palsy and significantly impacts quality of life, yet it often goes unrecognized and undertreated.

Prevalence​

"A meta-analysis revealed a high pain prevalence of 70% (95% CI 62-78) in adults with cerebral palsy."

This means 7 out of 10 adults with cerebral palsy experience chronic pain—a rate far higher than the general population.

Pain Locations​

Research from a North American registry found: "Back and weight-bearing joints of lower extremities were most frequently reported as painful."

Common pain locations include:

  • Back - from postural abnormalities and muscle imbalances
  • Hips - weight-bearing stress, possible dysplasia
  • Knees - gait abnormalities, crouch walking
  • Legs - 76% of pain reported in legs

Impact on Function and Quality of Life​

Pain in adults with cerebral palsy affects multiple domains:

"Those with moderate-to-severe pain showed increased depression, reduced social role satisfaction, and lower extremity function decline."

Key impacts:

  • Pain interference positively correlated with depression
  • Pain interference negatively correlated with physical function
  • Pain interference negatively correlated with social participation
  • Women report higher pain rates than men

Pain Severity​

Mean pain severity is 3.7/10 with mean interference of 3.5/10. Notably, pain severity does not significantly differ across GMFCS levels or age groups—meaning ambulatory and non-ambulatory individuals are equally affected.

Contributing Factors​

Pain in cerebral palsy results from multiple interacting factors:

FactorMechanism
SpasticityChronic muscle tension and spasms
Abnormal gaitStress on joints from compensatory movement patterns
Postural abnormalitiesScoliosis, hip displacement, contractures
OveruseCompensating for weakness in some muscle groups
AgingEarly degenerative changes (30s-40s vs 60s-70s in general population)

Treatment Approaches​

Nonpharmacologic Interventions (First-Line)​

Research shows "nonpharmacologic interventions were most commonly cited as beneficial" by adults with cerebral palsy.

  • Physical therapy - stretching, strengthening, positioning
  • Occupational therapy - activity modification, adaptive equipment
  • Aquatic therapy - reduced joint stress
  • Heat/cold therapy - muscle relaxation, inflammation reduction
  • Positioning - proper seating and sleeping positions

Pharmacologic Options​

  • Oral medications - NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, neuropathic pain agents
  • Botulinum toxin - for spasticity-related pain
  • Intrathecal baclofen - for severe, generalized spasticity

Spasticity Management​

Reducing spasticity often reduces pain. A meta-analysis found intrathecal baclofen achieved "a 40.25% reduction" in spasticity scores.

Screening Recommendations​

"Adults with cerebral palsy should be routinely screened for pain and treated accordingly."

Recommended approach:

  1. Screen for pain at every visit
  2. Use validated pain scales appropriate to communication ability
  3. Ask about pain location, severity, and interference
  4. Assess impact on function and participation
  5. Address underlying causes (spasticity, positioning, overuse)

Key Points​

  • Pain affects 70% of adults with cerebral palsy
  • Back and lower extremity joints are most commonly affected
  • Pain severity is similar across all GMFCS levels
  • Nonpharmacologic approaches are preferred first-line treatment
  • Routine screening is essential—pain is often underrecognized
  • Untreated pain leads to depression, reduced function, and social isolation
