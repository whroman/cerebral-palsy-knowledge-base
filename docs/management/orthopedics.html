Orthopedics & Hip Management in Adults with Cerebral Palsy

Adults with cerebral palsy frequently develop hip problems that cause pain and functional limitation. Unlike children, where surveillance can prevent displacement, adults often need treatment for established hip disease.

Hip Problems in Adults​

Hip subluxation and dislocation are common in cerebral palsy due to:

  • Muscle imbalance from spasticity
  • Abnormal positioning and lack of weight-bearing
  • Progressive displacement from childhood
  • Degenerative changes with aging

Risk by Motor Function​

Hip displacement correlates strongly with motor function level. Risk ranges from 1% with spastic hemiplegia to 75% with spastic quadriplegia.

GMFCS LevelHip Displacement Risk
I (walks without aids)~1%
II-III (walks with aids)Moderate
IV-V (wheelchair users)Up to 75%

Total Hip Arthroplasty (Hip Replacement)​

Evidence Summary​

A 2020 systematic review of 9 studies found positive outcomes for hip replacement in adults with cerebral palsy:

OutcomeResult
Pain relief87% achieved significant pain relief
Functional improvement79% improved function
5-year implant survival93.6%
10-15 year survival81-95%

"Total hip arthroplasty is a viable treatment option for adults with cerebral palsy."

Benefits​

  • Significant pain reduction
  • Improved positioning (sitting, lying)
  • Easier personal care and hygiene
  • Better quality of life
  • Long-term durability comparable to general population

Complications​

Adults with cerebral palsy have higher complication rates than typical hip replacement patients:

ComplicationRate
Dislocation7.5% (range 0-28%)
Periprosthetic fracture5.6% (range 0-21%)
Heterotopic ossification4.2% (range 0-38%)
Loosening3.7% (range 0-15%)
Infection2.1% (range 0-17%)

Revision rates vary from 0-26%, higher than the general population.

Indications for Surgery​

Common reasons for hip replacement in cerebral palsy:

  1. Painful degenerative joint disease (most common)
  2. Hip subluxation or dislocation
  3. Secondary osteoarthritis from dysplasia
  4. Failed treatment for fracture
  5. Failure of non-operative pain management

Hip Surveillance (Pediatric to Adult Transition)​

Hip surveillance programs monitor children for hip displacement. The AACPDM recommends:

  • Beginning by age 2 or at diagnosis
  • Regular clinical and radiographic monitoring
  • More frequent surveillance for GMFCS III-V
  • Continuing until skeletal maturity

Gap in adult care: Surveillance protocols focus on children. Adults may present with advanced hip disease that was missed or progressed after pediatric care ended.

Other Orthopedic Issues​

Scoliosis​

46% of adults with cerebral palsy have scoliosis (see Secondary Conditions).

Management considerations:

  • Seating and positioning assessment
  • Bracing for comfort (not curve correction in adults)
  • Surgical evaluation for severe, progressive curves
  • Monitor respiratory impact

Osteoporosis​

Adults with cerebral palsy have elevated fracture risk due to:

  • Limited weight-bearing
  • Anticonvulsant medications
  • Vitamin D deficiency
  • Low muscle mass

Prevention:

  • Weight-bearing when possible
  • Vitamin D supplementation
  • Calcium intake monitoring
  • Earlier bone density screening than general population

Specialist Team​

SpecialistRole
Orthopedic surgeonSurgical evaluation and treatment
PhysiatristNon-operative management, coordination
Physical therapistPre/post-operative rehabilitation
Pain specialistNon-operative pain management

Decision-Making Considerations​

When considering hip surgery in adults with cerebral palsy:

Factors favoring surgery:

  • Significant pain uncontrolled by medications
  • Pain affecting sleep, positioning, quality of life
  • Progressive functional decline
  • Ability to participate in post-operative care

Challenges:

  • Higher complication rates than general population
  • Need for specialized surgical expertise
  • Post-operative rehabilitation may be complex
  • May need general anesthesia considerations

Key Points​

  • Hip problems are common in adults with cerebral palsy, especially GMFCS IV-V
  • Total hip arthroplasty provides significant pain relief (87%) and functional improvement (79%)
  • Complication rates are higher than general population but manageable
  • Implant survival is comparable to standard populations (81-95% at 10-15 years)
  • Adults may present with advanced hip disease missed in pediatric care
  • Orthopedic surgeon experienced with neuromuscular conditions is essential
