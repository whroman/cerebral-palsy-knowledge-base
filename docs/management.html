Management

Cerebral palsy management requires coordination across multiple specialists. Without a central coordinator, care becomes fragmented—each specialist addresses their piece without seeing the whole picture.

The Coordination Problem​

Adults with cerebral palsy often see:

  • Neurologist (seizures, spasticity medications)
  • Orthopedist (bone/joint issues)
  • PT/OT/SLP (therapy services)
  • Primary care (general health)
  • Various subspecialists

Without coordination:

  • Treatments may conflict
  • Gaps in care go unnoticed
  • No one tracks overall function
  • Aging-related changes missed

The Physiatrist Solution​

Physiatrists (Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation physicians) are trained to:

  • Coordinate care across specialties
  • Assess function holistically
  • Manage spasticity
  • Oversee rehabilitation services
  • Track changes over time

A physiatrist should be the central coordinator for adult cerebral palsy care.

Key Management Areas​

AreaFocusSpecialists
SpasticityTone management, botulinum toxin, medicationsPhysiatrist, neurologist
MobilityGait, equipment, transfersPhysiatrist, PT
PainChronic pain (70% affected)Physiatrist, pain specialist
Nutrition/FeedingDysphagia (85% affected), aspiration preventionSLP, dietitian, GI
CommunicationSpeech, AACSLP
Daily livingIndependence, adaptationsOT

What to Do​

  1. Find a physiatrist - preferably one experienced with cerebral palsy
  2. Establish as primary coordinator - not just another specialist
  3. Bring records from all providers - enable coordination
  4. Request annual functional review - track changes over time
